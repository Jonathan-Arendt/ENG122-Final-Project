<p>
Rethinking Nuclear Power
</p>
<p>
Many people are familiar with the various methods of generating electricity, from solar and wind power to coal and natural gas; these are just a few of the methods in use today. However, these methods have downsides, from wind and solar being intermittent, to coal and natural gas resulting in carbon emissions. These drawbacks pose challenges for generating clean, substantial energy. However, one technology already exists to fulfill this purpose; however, it has fallen by the wayside due to public perception and political resistance. This technology, nuclear energy, was led by the US. However, after nuclear accidents, such as Chernobyl, Three Mile Island, and Fukushima Daiichi, the US stopped designing new nuclear power plants (World Nuclear Association, 2026d). However, the US should increase the use of nuclear power because it would increase the share of clean energy in the power mix.
</p>

<h2>
Background
</h2>
<p>
When a person hears the term nuclear energy, they likely will immediately think of the Manhattan Project, with the development of nuclear weapons; however, nuclear energy consists of more than that. According to the International Atomic Energy Agency (Galindo, 2025), Nuclear energy refers to the form of energy released when an atomic nucleus is split or fused, also known as fission and fusion. While both are forms of nuclear energy, nuclear fission is more commonly associated with the power it produces, as nuclear power via fission has existed since the 1950s-1960s, whereas nuclear fusion is still being developed. For this reason, ‘nuclear energy’ or ‘nuclear power’, for the purposes of this essay, refer to the power created by nuclear fission. 
</p>
<p>
As mentioned, the first stable fission reaction for power generation occurred shortly after World War II. In Idaho, the Experimental Breeder Reactor (EBR-1), designed and operated by Argonne National Laboratory, took the title of the first nuclear reactor to produce electricity (World Nuclear Association, 2025). This reactor was not specifically designed for power generation, but rather for “breeding” plutonium, meaning that the uranium was converted to plutonium, and the energy was a byproduct of this nuclear reaction. The first nuclear reactors designed for power generation were pressurized water reactors (PWRs), which were first used for submarines as early as 1954 and for surface vessels by 1959 (World Nuclear Association, 2025). PWRs were the first type of nuclear reactor used for commercial power generation, with the first commercial reactor starting power generation in 1960 (World Nuclear Association, 2025).
</p>
<p>
While the specifics of the power plant type may seem unnecessary, the differences between two reactors and the method of power generation are important when discussing nuclear reactor safety. Because of this, there are two other types of nuclear reactors worth mentioning. While the first commercial PWR reactor was being constructed, research was underway for the boiling water reactor (BWR), developed by the Argonne National Laboratory (World Nuclear Association, 2025). The key difference between a PWR and a BWR is that a PWR reactor heats water under pressure, preventing it from boiling and turning to steam. This heated water is used to boil a second, isolated loop of water, where the resulting steam is then used to generate power. In contrast, the BWR boils the water inside the reactor and uses the steam directly from the reactor to generate power. (Duke Energy, 2012)
</p>
<p>
The second relevant reactor is the RBMK (reaktor bolshoy moshchnosty kanalny, high-power channel reactor) reactor developed by the Soviet Union in 1964 (World Nuclear Association, 2026e). This type of reactor, also known as a light-water graphite reactor, operated similarly to a BWR reactor; however, it relied on graphite to moderate, or regulate, the nuclear reaction. The type of reactor was only used in the Soviet Union, and “the combination of graphite moderator and water coolant is found in no other power reactors in the world” (World Nuclear Association, 2026e, para. 2). The failings in the RBMK reactor design is what would eventually lead to the Chernobyl Accident in 1986, the worst nuclear accident in history, in both human and environmental impact (World Nuclear Association, 2026a). 
</p>

<h2>
Argument
</h2>
<h2>
Nuclear Energy Safety and Public Perception
</h2>

<p>
While public opinion played a key role in the reduction of nuclear power plants in the United States, the main factor driving this position was nuclear plant safety. While the nuclear accidents and incidents listed above are mainly responsible for this negative stigma, nuclear safety is always at the forefront of those responsible for the operation of the nuclear plant. From technological advancements and extensive consideration in plant design to the safety-first work culture at these plants, the plants’ safe operation is ensured.
</p>
<p>
While accidents, such as Three Mile Island and Chernobyl, are the main instances in which a lack of nuclear safety led to accidents, they have also driven many of the advancements in nuclear safety. However, it is important to discuss these accidents and similar incidents in their own section, including their causes, outcomes, and resulting changes in the nuclear industry. They are discussed in full in the section on Nuclear Accidents and Advancements in Safety. 
</p>
<p>
Although major accidents often result in important design changes, many safety improvements are implemented proactively rather than reactively. Passive safety features are one such advancement in modern nuclear plant designs (International Atomic Energy Agency, n.d.). In PWR and BWR designs, a lack of flowing water through the reactor will result in a meltdown, where the nuclear fuel becomes so hot from nuclear fusion that it melts. To prevent this from occurring during normal operation, cooling water is continually pumped through the reactor. An electric pump is an active safety system since if the plant loses power, the necessary water stops flowing. To prevent this, many plant designs include a tank above the reactor that, in the event of an emergency, continually dumps water into the reactor. Since gravity maintains this flow rather than electricity, it is a passive safety system. 
</p>
<p>
Another example of passive safety systems is the insertion and removal of both control rods and the nuclear fuel. Instead of using a motor or piston to insert the control rods or nuclear fuel, reactors are instead designed so that the piston raises the control rod. In the event of a power failure, the control rods fall back down, further helping prevent a nuclear meltdown. Because of these and many other passive safety systems, every time a nuclear plant loses power, a potential nuclear accident is prevented. 
</p>
<p>
Furthermore, while nuclear safety is most often associated with public safety, the safety of plant operators is also a key concern. One of the key methods for reducing exposure to nuclear radiation is the ALARA (As-Low-As-Reasonably-Achievable) principle (Okrent, 1987). While this method is not exclusive to nuclear power (which is why medical X-rays are limited to medically necessary exposures), the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) uses ALARA, along with strict dosage limits, to prevent unnecessary radiation exposure. ALARA focuses on three core principles to reduce radiation exposure: Time, Distance, and Shielding. The duration of exposure is important, as not all radiation exposure is life-threatening (for example, medical X-rays), but limiting the duration at a certain rate is important. Distance and Shielding, on the other hand, do not limit the time; rather, they reduce the dose. For example, say a technician needs to perform a task in a radioactive environment, and that task will take exactly 30 minutes; the time of exposure cannot be strictly limited. Instead, minimizing the distance or adding lead shielding both reduce the received dose. 
</p>
<p>
As a result, from proactive reactor design to government policy and regulation, every aspect of a nuclear power plant is controlled. This leads to a work culture where safety is the primary concern. Moreover, while the discussion could include further examples of safety policies, such as an open-door policy for safety concerns or the extensive security measures plants use to prevent unauthorized access, it is clear that safety is the primary concern, from government regulators overseeing the plant to the operations responsible for its operation. 
</p>

<h2>
Nuclear Power as a Low-Emission Energy Source
</h2>

<p>
 While nuclear safety is the primary concern, nuclear power as a clean energy source is the primary benefit. When discussing clean energy, most of the conversation centers on carbon dioxide (CO2), the primary greenhouse gas emitted by burning fossil fuels (U.S. Energy Information Administration, 2024). However, there are many other emissions that not only increase greenhouse gas emissions but are also toxic and environmentally harmful. Such emissions include Sulfur Dioxide, which contributes to acid rain, Nitrogen Oxide, which increases smog, and Mercury, which is linked to neurological damage (Vieira, 2020; U.S. Energy Information Administration, 2024). While regulations have been put into place by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), many of these negative emissions are still released, albeit in significantly lower quantities. 
</p>
<p>
In contrast, nuclear energy does not have such emissions during plant operation, with the only byproduct being nuclear waste. Many people, however, view nuclear waste as a significant negative. Despite this negative perception, nuclear waste is actually a solved issue, and this will be discussed in depth in the subsection regarding Nuclear Waste Management.
</p>
<p>
Despite the clear benefits of nuclear power as a clean energy source, many countries, such as the U.S., have stopped adding new nuclear power plants, and some countries, such as Germany, have initiated a complete phase-out of nuclear power. In their article investigating CO2 emissions from nuclear phase-out in Germany, Loiacono et al. (2025) found a significant increase in pollution due to the increased reliance on fossil fuels. While a large portion of this pollution can be mitigated by hydro, solar, and wind power, these alternative power sources are not short-term solutions. Hydro power is limited by geographical location, while wind and solar have significantly lower power generation. This leads to the next topic, the demand for nuclear power. 
</p>
<p>
Nuclear Power and Growing Energy Demand in the United States
</p>
<p>
With the rise of electric cars and AI data centers in the US, demand has risen significantly, with some projections of a 9,000% increase in electric demand over the next 10 to 15 years (Hendricks, 2025). With such a sharp increase in energy demand, suitable power solutions are needed, which are both efficient and net-beneficial. This leads to two main metrics for comparing various energy sources. 
</p>
<p>
The first metric is land-use intensity of electricity, measured in Hectares per Terawatt-hour per year. It measures the amount of land needed to produce a given amount of energy in a year, comparing different energy sources by energy generation density. For example, the median land-use intensity for coal, solar, hydro, and natural falls around 1000 ha/TWh/year (+/-250). Furthermore, the median for wind (including turbine spacing) exceeds 10,000 ha/TWh/year. In contrast, nuclear power is the most space-efficient, with the median falling at 7.1 ha/TWh/year. This means that, for an equivalent amount of power generation, coal, solar, and hydro would, on average, require 100x the space of nuclear power, with wind requiring 1000x the space (Blomqvist, 2022). These numbers show that nuclear power is far more space-efficient, allowing for larger increases in power demand.
</p>
<p>
The second key factor in deciding which power source to use is the positives and drawbacks of each source. Natural gas and coal, while using readily available resources, release CO2 and other chemical pollutants during combustion, as mentioned previously, but they are also fossil fuels. These fuels are created from carbon in fossils by compression at high temperature and pressure underground, making them nonrenewable. While hydro power is a green, renewable energy source, it is limited by the geography of lakes, rivers, and cities. Similarly, solar and wind power rely on weather conditions. The benefit of these technologies is that they are both renewable resources. In contrast, nuclear power is not renewable, as the fuel needed does not naturally replenish itself on Earth (Vieira, 2020). However, nuclear power is clean and does not depend on weather; however, it may be less efficient. Most plant designs use water from nearby lakes or rivers or rely on cooling towers that use ambient air to condense the steam back into water. As such, the plant’s efficiency depends on the difference between external and internal temperatures, meaning that cooler water or lower temperatures increase the plant’s efficiency (World Nuclear Association, 2020).
</p>

<h2>
Nuclear Waste Management
</h2>

<p>
As cited in the article regarding the pros and cons of various energy types, Vieira (2020) lists nuclear waste as a con of nuclear energy. Flavin and Lenssen take this a step further, stating, “Additional difficulties have stemmed from the [worldwide] failure to develop a safe, permanent means of disposing of long-lived nuclear waste” (2002, para. 6). While Flavin and Lenssen wrote this article almost two and a half decades ago, this is still a popular position, as illustrated by this issue being listed in the article by Vieira. However, the issue at hand for the US is not limited to technology, but rather a lack of decision by the US government on the disposal of high-level waste. 
</p>
<p>
There are two categories of radioactive waste generated by a nuclear power plant: high-level waste (HLW) and low-level waste(LLW). LLW is radiologically contaminated waste (trash), such as gloves and clothes, mops and rags, or tools and equipment. (Interestingly, this includes medical materials from treating those with radiation sickness, and even syringes and needles used for administrations of chemotherapy.) LLW is waste that has a half-life of 30 years. (Half-life is the scientific term for the statistical amount of time it takes for half of a nuclear sample to decay.) LLW is stored in near-surface disposal sites, at or below ground level (at depths of tens of meters). Such sites are already implemented in many countries, including the US (World Nuclear Organization, 2026f).
</p>
<p>
The other relevant category of waste is HLW. HLW is what is most commonly associated with the phrase “nuclear waste”. This is the category of waste that includes materials with half-lives ranging from 30 years to 2.144 million years. An important note, while it can take millions of years for the waste to no longer be radioactive, the radioactivity drops significantly within the first few years. Spent fuel typically rides in the spent fuel pool, under meters of water (which blocks radiation) for several years as it cools to a temperature at which it can be stored in dry casks. At this point, the dry casks can be stored for the long term above ground or buried using deep geological disposal. Many other countries have already adopted polices using various methods of deep geological disposal; however, the US has not. As such, the issue is not whether the technology to dispose of HLW exists, but rather whether the government should adopt a policy on the method of disposal (World Nuclear Organization, 2026f). 
</p>

<h2>
Nuclear Accidents and Advancements in Safety
</h2>

<p>
Nuclear accidents are one of the primary concerns when it comes to nuclear safety. It is easy to focus on the major accidents, thinking that this will happen to the nuclear plant near me. However, in many cases, these accidents had underlying issues that are no longer relevant due to the safety measures implemented in nuclear plants worldwide. As such, it is important to discuss these accidents individually, so it can be better understood why they happened, what factors made the disasters widespread, and how safety has improved to prevent them from happening again.
</p>

<h3>
Three Mile Island
</h3>

<p>
Individuals familiar with the history of nuclear accidents may recognize the name of Three Mile Island. While Three Mile Island was not a major nuclear disaster, like Chernobyl or Fukushima Daichi, Three Mile Island was instead a media disaster. Three Mile Island is a PWR nuclear power plant located near Harrisburg, Pennsylvania, in the US. In 1979, Three Mile Island experienced a cooling malfunction, which resulted in a partial meltdown of the #2 reactor. After the reactor shut down, the valve that allows water to leave the reactor should have closed within 10 seconds. Due to a mechanical failure, the valve did not close, allowing coolant to escape the reactor. The operators initiated the high-pressure injection pump to replace the escaping water; however, due to human error, they believed the issue was resolved, and it was not until 2 hours later that they closed a block valve, which stopped the water release. As a result of the delayed reaction, the gaseous, radioactive byproduct of krypton accumulated in the reactor, and operators were forced to vent the gas into the atmosphere. After the krypton was vented, a hydrogen gas bubble began to build at the top of the reactor, which the NRC believed could explode (World Nuclear Organization, 2026g). 
</p>
<p>
While plant operators worked to dissipate the hydrogen bubble, plant managers held several press conferences, making it unclear whether venting the krypton gas was dangerous, whether the hydrogen would cause the reactor to explode, and whether the NRC had called for an evacuation. This miscommunication resulted from conversations between officials and those unaware of the plant’s actual condition. It was later found that despite the mass panic and confusion, the amount of radiation to a person living within 10 miles was no more than that of a chest X-ray (World Nuclear Organization, 2026g). 
</p>
<p>
As a result, there were reforms to the training of all plant operators across the states, as this was the primary factor in the human error that led to the 2-hour delay in resolving the cooling issue. Also resulting from this incident were changes in the standard for the safety and reliability of mechanical parts. After the incident, the NRC required plants to meet higher standards, implement passive safety systems, and ensure their performance under various unplanned conditions (World Nuclear Organization, 2026g).
</p>

<h3>
Fukushima Diichi
</h3>

<p>
Fukushima Diichi, a BWR, consisted of 4 reactor units located on the Japanese coast. On 11 March 2011, the plant was hit by a 9.0 magnitude earthquake, causing the reactor to shut down as designed. No serious damage occurred as a result of the earthquake. However, all six external power supplies, which connect the plant to the grid when it is not running, were lost during the earthquake, causing the backup generators to kick in. 41 minutes later, a tsunami struck the plant, damaging the backup batteries, coolant pumps, and backup generators. To make a long story short, this led to damage to the core and the reactor pressure vessel, which caused the release of radioactive gas and a hydrogen explosion in several reactors. There is much more to this story, including issues with the clean-up, unclear communication from the Japanese Government, and fallout effects from the plant. For more information, see the World Nuclear Association (2026b) article. However, this brief synopsis of the first few hours suggests that everything worked correctly and that the disaster still happened, but that is not the case.
</p>
<p>
The original design of the Fukushima Daichi plant accounted for a tsunami no greater than 10 meters above sea level. “In the last century, there have been eight tsunamis in the region with maximum amplitudes at origin above 10 meters (World Nuclear Organization, 2026b, para. 9).” 18 years prior to the disaster, scientific knowledge indicated a high likelihood that a tsunami of 15.7 meters would strike the Fukushima Daichi plant. However, despite the evidence that changes were needed, no action was taken by the plant staff, the plant owner, Tepco, or the Japanese government, the Nuclear & Industrial Safety Agency (NISA). Had NISA implemented changes in accordance with the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) guidelines, the generators, battery back-ups, and back-up cooling pumps would have prevented the disaster. To prevent bureaucratic breakdowns from recurring, the Japanese government split NISA into separate departments, one dedicated to Nuclear Safety and the other to Industrial Safety. There have been no deaths as a result of the accident and no recorded cases of radiation sickness; however, that was not the case with Chernobyl (World Nuclear Organization, 2026a; World Nuclear Organization, 2026b). 
</p>

<h3>
Chernobyl
</h3>

<p>
Chernobyl, located outside of Pripyat, Ukraine, during the Soviet Era. On 25 April 1986, after running tests on the newly built reactor to obtain the necessary certifications, the plant operators began a test, running the #4 reactor at low power. While lowering power to the desired test threshold, the reactor’s reactivity dropped too quickly, leading the staff to believe the reactor was stalled. As the operator pressed the SCRAM button (the emergency stop that immediately lowers all control rods), and as the control rods dropped into place, the reactor's thermal output jumped 10x beyond the reactor's design limit. This led to an explosion killing two workers immediately, as the 1000-ton cover plate of the reactor became partially detached, leading to the fuel cells rupturing. As steam rushed into the voids, a second explosion occurred, launching approximately 1200 tons of graphite, which was radiologically contaminated due to its proximity to the fuel cells. The reactor continued to burn for hours, releasing smoke contaminated with radioactive particles that fell out as the plume traveled across the country (World Nuclear Organization, 2026a). 
</p>
<p>
Again, this summary does not tell the whole story, such as why inserting the control rods, the part of the reactor that is meant to stop the nuclear reaction, caused the reactor to increase instead of decrease. In RBMK reactors, the control rods were tipped with graphite to displace the water; however, this displaced the water, leading to an initial increase in reactivity. So when all 211 rods were inserted at once, the reaction increased, leading to the eventual calamity. This, however, was on only several key design flaws that created the situation, which was triggered by the control rods. The plant’s totalitarian leadership worsened the situation resulting from the two explosions. All of this resulted in the immediate death of 30 operators and firemen, two of whom died from the explosion, and 28 died within 3 months due to radiation sickness. There were a total of 237 reported cases of Acute Radiation Syndrome, of which only 134 were verified. Furthermore, there were over 20,000 reported cases of thyroid cancer in those who were 18 and under at the time of the accident (World Nuclear Organization, 2026a; World Nuclear Organization, 2026e). 
</p>
<p>
While there is much more to tell regarding the investigation, the efforts to extinguish the fire, and the eventual trial of the leadership ultimately responsible for the disaster, the key takeaways are that the accident was caused by two main factors: poor reactor design and totalitarian leadership. In the US and other countries worldwide, this incident led to increased passive safety systems, a change in work culture at power plants, and greater consideration of reactor design and how it operates (World Nuclear Organization, 2026a). 
</p>

<h2>
Conclusion
</h2>

<p>
The United States needs to increase its use of nuclear power as a clean and reliable energy source. Yet people tend to fear what they do not understand, often believing that the technology is too dangerous to adopt. This pattern is evident worldwide, with the US experiencing a major halt of the construction of new nuclear power plants after the Three Mile Island incident (World Nuclear Association, 2026d), with Germany halting construction after Chernobyl and decommissioning existing plants after Fukushima Daiichi (World Nuclear Organization, 2026c), and with Italy voting to decommission all of its nuclear power plants one year after Fukushima Daiichi (Adendroff, 2011). However, these incidents were not caused by unsafe technology. Rather, they were mainly due to human error caused by poor training, dictatorial leadership, and an unwillingness to upgrade the plant's safety in conjunction with the design flaws present. 
</p>
<p>
Thomas Eiden summarizes the issue well: “You are in more danger each day when you get into your car than you would be if you entered one of these power plants” (2017, para. 8). While one could argue that many people were affected long term (referencing the studies which indicate 20,000 people had thyroid cancer who were under the age of 18 at the time of Chernobyl), the total deaths caused by aviation crashes between 1982 and 2018 were a total of 47,242 deaths (which does not include the over 17,000 total severe injuries and over 28,000 total minor injuries) (Panish | Shea | Ravipudi LLP., 2025), and over 37,000 people die in car crashes each year (Eiden, 2017). More people have died in car crashes each year and in all plane crashes than those likely to have experienced long-term effects from the Chornobyl Accident. Despite the numbers showing that cars and planes are more dangerous, as Thomas Eiden points out, nuclear power is far more regulated than either of these. 
</p>
<p>
Furthermore, it is easy to argue that nuclear power is not worth the risk, whereas planes and cars are a necessity; however, this argument also does not hold. Nuclear power is a clean source of energy, producing no carbon emissions during operation, and takes up a fraction of the space compared to clean renewables such as wind and solar. With current power demand increasing due to AI data centers and self-driving/electric cars, it is not sufficient to build more turbines or solar plants. Currently, much of the world relies on fossil fuels to meet base demand; however, the emissions produced are far from ideal. This trade-off is why the US needs to increase its utilization of nuclear power. 
</p>
<p>
However, it is insufficient to say that the government needs to build more nuclear plants. There are three things that need to change in the US before this technology will be adopted. First is public perception, as without support from the populace, leaders within the government will not change policies and practices required to increase nuclear power. This is the goal of this essay: to inform people about nuclear power, explain why it is needed, and dispel the fears associated with the technology. 
</p>
<p>
Once there is broad support for nuclear power, policymakers are empowered to change the necessary policies, which leads to the second change: the US needs to adopt a policy for the management and storage of HLW. As previously discussed, nuclear waste is not an unsolved problem; however, the US is the only country without policies in place to manage HLW long-term. Since the use of nuclear power declined in the US after Three Mile Island, there has been no need to implement new policies regarding HLW, and, without a policy, it becomes a point of contention in the development of new plants. The opposition often cites the US’s inability to manage nuclear waste as a key reason nuclear power should not be utilized; however, once policies are created, this is no longer an issue. 
</p>
<p>
Only then can society start to change the final thing: no longer relying on fossil fuels and focusing on creating new plants. Once reliance on coal and gas is reduced and the US can support the demand from electric vehicles and AI, society will build energy independence and no longer be reliant on oil imports from the Middle East. As such, it is imperative that society no longer focus on the catastrophes that have occurred but rather look towards the future. But this all starts with education on the safety of nuclear power. The choice is no longer whether nations can use nuclear power safely, but rather whether society is willing to move beyond fear and use it to build a sustainable future for the nation and its citizens. 
</p>
